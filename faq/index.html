Frequently Asked Questions

Expert answers to common questions about California water wells, permits, costs, and maintenance

Well Depth & Drilling

How deep should a well be in California?

Well depth varies significantly across California due to diverse geology and groundwater conditions:

  • Coastal areas: 50-200 feet typical
  • Sacramento Valley: 100-400 feet typical
  • San Joaquin Valley: 200-800+ feet typical
  • Mountain/foothill regions: 100-500 feet typical
  • Desert regions: 200-600+ feet typical

Use our free Well Lookup Tool to find depths of wells near your property. This gives the most accurate estimate for your specific location.

How much does it cost to drill a well in California?

Total well installation typically costs $15,000-$35,000, broken down as:

  • Drilling: $25-$65 per foot (deeper wells cost more per foot)
  • Casing: $15-$35 per foot for steel casing
  • Pump & equipment: $2,000-$8,000 depending on depth and flow needs
  • Permits: $200-$800 depending on county
  • Water testing: $100-$500

For accurate pump sizing for your well depth, try our Pump Calculator.

How long does it take to drill a well?

The timeline for a new well typically includes:

  • Permit approval: 1-4 weeks
  • Drilling: 1-5 days (depending on depth and conditions)
  • Well development: 1-2 days
  • Pump installation: 1-2 days
  • Final inspection: 1-2 weeks

Total time from permit application to flowing water: 4-8 weeks on average.

Permits & Regulations

Do I need a permit to drill a well in California?

Yes. All new water wells in California require a permit from your county Environmental Health Department or local water agency. This applies to:

  • New domestic wells
  • Agricultural irrigation wells
  • Well deepening or modification
  • Replacement wells

Contact your county's permit office for specific requirements and fees.

Can I drill my own well in California?

Property owners can drill wells on their own land, but there are important considerations:

  • You still need a county permit
  • The well must meet all state and county construction standards
  • You're responsible for proper sealing to prevent contamination
  • Professional drilling is recommended for wells deeper than 50 feet

Most homeowners hire a licensed C-57 well drilling contractor due to the specialized equipment and expertise required.

What are setback requirements for wells in California?

California requires minimum distances between wells and potential contamination sources:

  • Septic tanks: 50-100 feet minimum
  • Septic leach fields: 100-150 feet minimum
  • Property lines: 5-10 feet (varies by county)
  • Buildings: 5-10 feet
  • Animal enclosures: 50-100 feet

Check with your county for specific setback requirements as they vary.

Well Pumps & Equipment

What size pump do I need for my well?

Pump sizing depends on several factors:

  • Well depth: Deeper wells need more powerful pumps
  • Static water level: Distance from surface to water
  • Flow rate needed: 5-10 GPM for most homes
  • Pressure requirements: Usually 40-60 PSI
  • Pipe size and distance: Affects friction loss

Our free Pump Calculator helps you determine the right pump size for your specific situation.

How long do well pumps last?

Well pump lifespan depends on the type and usage:

  • Submersible pumps: 8-15 years average
  • Jet pumps: 10-15 years average
  • Pressure tanks: 10-15 years

Factors affecting pump life include water quality (sand/sediment), cycling frequency, and proper sizing. Oversized or undersized pumps wear out faster.

When should I replace my well pump?

Warning signs that your pump may need replacement:

  • Frequent cycling (turning on/off repeatedly)
  • Decreased water pressure
  • Air sputtering from faucets
  • Sandy or muddy water
  • Unusually high electric bills
  • Strange noises from the well
  • Complete loss of water

If your pump is over 10 years old and showing these signs, replacement is often more cost-effective than repair.

Water Quality & Testing

How often should I test my well water?

California recommends the following testing schedule:

  • Bacteria (coliform): Annually
  • Nitrates: Annually (more often with infants in home)
  • Full mineral panel: Every 3-5 years
  • After any repairs: Test for bacteria
  • If taste/odor changes: Test immediately

Testing costs $50-$300 depending on what's tested. Contact your county health department for certified labs.

Is well water safe to drink in California?

Most California well water is safe, but some areas have natural or man-made contamination concerns:

  • Arsenic: Naturally occurring in some foothill and valley areas
  • Nitrates: Common in agricultural areas
  • Bacteria: Can occur if well isn't properly sealed
  • Hard water: Common but not a health concern

Always test new wells before drinking. Treatment systems are available for most contaminants. For professional guidance, consult a well water specialist.

What causes low well water yield?

Common causes of decreased well production:

  • Dropping water table: Drought or increased groundwater use
  • Pump problems: Worn impellers or motor issues
  • Clogged screens: Mineral or bacteria buildup
  • Well collapse: Casing failure in older wells
  • Aquifer depletion: Regional groundwater decline

A well inspection can diagnose the problem. Solutions range from pump repair to well deepening or drilling a new well.

What do I do if my well runs dry?

If your well stops producing water:

  • Check the obvious: Circuit breaker, pressure switch, electrical connections
  • Wait and test: If during heavy use, water may recover in a few hours
  • Call a professional: Could be pump failure, not a dry well
  • Options if truly dry:
    • Deepen existing well
    • Drill a new, deeper well
    • Hydrofracturing (in rock formations)
    • Install storage tank for low-yield wells
