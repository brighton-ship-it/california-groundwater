1. Low Water Pressure

üîç Symptoms

Weak flow from faucets and showers, sprinklers don't reach full distance, pressure drops when multiple fixtures are used

Possible Causes

  • Pressure tank issues: Waterlogged tank or failed bladder
  • Pressure switch problems: Incorrect settings or worn contacts
  • Clogged pipes or filters: Mineral buildup restricting flow
  • Pump wear: Worn impellers reducing output
  • Dropping water table: Pump struggling to lift water

‚úÖ Solutions

  1. Check pressure tank: Tap the tank‚Äîif it sounds solid/heavy, it may be waterlogged. Pressure tanks should have some air space.
  2. Inspect pressure switch: Settings should be 40/60 or 30/50 PSI. Look for burned contacts.
  3. Replace filters: Sediment filters should be changed every 3-6 months.
  4. Test pump output: Time how long to fill a 5-gallon bucket. Less than 1 minute = healthy flow.

If your well is over 10 years old and pressure has gradually declined, the pump may need replacement. Use our Pump Calculator to determine the right size for your replacement pump.

2. No Water at All

üîç Symptoms

Turn on faucet and nothing comes out, pump doesn't seem to be running

Before You Panic

Complete water loss is alarming, but it's often a simple electrical issue rather than a dry well. Check these first:

  1. Circuit breaker: Check the breaker panel‚Äîwell pumps have their own breaker
  2. Pressure switch: Look for the gray box on your pressure tank. Tap it gently or flip the lever if it has one.
  3. GFCI outlet: Some well controls are on GFCI circuits that may have tripped

‚úÖ If It's Not Electrical

  • Check well cap: Ensure it's secure and wiring is intact
  • Listen at the wellhead: You should hear the pump running when pressure is low
  • Call a professional: Could be a failed pump, broken drop pipe, or actual water loss

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: If your well has truly gone dry (confirmed by a professional), don't run the pump‚Äîit can burn out quickly without water to cool it. Options include well deepening, hydrofracturing, or drilling a new well.

3. Air Sputtering from Faucets

üîç Symptoms

Air bursts mixed with water, "spitting" faucets, inconsistent flow

Common Causes

  • Dropping water level: Pump is drawing air intermittently
  • Leaking drop pipe: Air entering through crack in pipe below ground
  • Failing pump: Internal seals allowing air in
  • Low water table: Seasonal or drought-related decline

‚úÖ Solutions

Air in your water system usually indicates the pump is too close to the current water level. Solutions include:

  • Lowering the pump deeper in the well (if well is deep enough)
  • Reducing water usage to let the well recover
  • Installing a storage tank system for low-yield wells
  • Deepening the well

4. Pump Short Cycling

üîç Symptoms

Pump turns on and off frequently (every few seconds to minutes), even when no water is being used

Short cycling is one of the most common‚Äîand most damaging‚Äîwell problems. It causes premature pump wear and high electric bills.

Primary Causes

  1. Waterlogged pressure tank: The bladder has failed and the tank is full of water with no air cushion
  2. Leaking check valve: Water flows back into the well, dropping pressure
  3. Pressure switch issues: Stuck contacts or incorrect settings
  4. Plumbing leak: Hidden leak constantly draining pressure

‚úÖ Diagnosis & Fix

  1. Check pressure tank air charge: Turn off power, drain tank, check air pressure with tire gauge at the Schrader valve. Should be 2 PSI below cut-in pressure (e.g., 38 PSI for 40/60 setting).
  2. If no air charge holds: Bladder is failed‚Äîreplace the pressure tank ($200-$500)
  3. Check for leaks: Turn off all fixtures and watch pressure gauge. If it drops, you have a leak.

5. Dirty or Sandy Water

üîç Symptoms

Brown, orange, or cloudy water; sediment in toilet tanks; sand in faucet aerators

Causes & Solutions

  • Iron bacteria: Orange/brown slime, musty odor ‚Üí Well shock chlorination
  • Rust from casing: Reddish-brown water ‚Üí May need well rehabilitation or casing repair
  • Sand infiltration: Sand in water ‚Üí Screen failure, pump set too low, or pump too powerful for formation
  • Recent drilling nearby: Temporary sediment disturbance ‚Üí Usually clears in days to weeks

‚úÖ Short-term Solutions

  • Install sediment filter at pressure tank (change frequently)
  • Run water until clear before using for household needs
  • Flush water heater more frequently

Long-term Solutions

  • Well rehabilitation (professional cleaning)
  • Screen repair or replacement
  • Properly sized replacement pump

6. Rotten Egg Smell (Hydrogen Sulfide)

üîç Symptoms

Strong sulfur/"rotten egg" odor from water, especially hot water; sometimes black staining

This is one of the most common water quality complaints in California wells. While unpleasant, hydrogen sulfide at typical levels isn't dangerous.

Causes

  • Sulfur bacteria in well: Naturally occurring bacteria that produce hydrogen sulfide gas
  • Groundwater chemistry: Some aquifers have natural sulfur compounds
  • Water heater: If smell is only in hot water, the anode rod may be reacting with bacteria

‚úÖ Solutions

  • Hot water only: Replace water heater anode rod with aluminum/zinc type, or raise temperature to 140¬∞F temporarily to kill bacteria
  • All water: Well shock chlorination (kills sulfur bacteria)
  • Persistent problem: Install aeration system or chlorination/carbon filtration system

7. Hard Water / Scale Buildup

üîç Symptoms

White scale on faucets and showerheads, spots on dishes, soap doesn't lather well, stiff laundry

Hard water is extremely common in California wells. It's caused by high calcium and magnesium content‚Äînot harmful to drink but tough on plumbing and appliances.

‚úÖ Solutions

  • Water softener: Most effective solution, uses salt to remove hardness minerals ($500-$2,000 installed)
  • Salt-free conditioner: Doesn't remove minerals but reduces scaling ($300-$1,000)
  • Vinegar treatments: Regular cleaning of fixtures with white vinegar dissolves scale

8. Unusually High Electric Bills

üîç Symptoms

Electric bill jumps significantly without other explanation, pump seems to run constantly

Common Causes

  • Short cycling pump: Starting uses more power than running‚Äîfrequent starts = high bills
  • Water leak: Running toilet, irrigation leak, or broken pipe causing constant pumping
  • Worn pump: Struggling to maintain pressure, running longer than necessary
  • Incorrect pump size: Oversized pump working harder than needed

‚úÖ Diagnosis Steps

  1. Turn off all water fixtures and check if pump still runs
  2. Check for running toilets (add food coloring to tank)
  3. Inspect irrigation system for leaks
  4. Listen for pump cycling frequency

A properly functioning well pump should run only when water is actually being used. If yours runs constantly, investigate immediately to prevent pump burnout.

9. Strange Pump Noises

üîç Symptoms

Grinding, clicking, humming without starting, or vibration sounds from well or pressure tank area

What Different Sounds Mean

  • Humming but not starting: Capacitor failure or seized motor‚Äîneeds professional repair
  • Clicking from pressure switch: Worn contacts trying to engage‚Äîreplace pressure switch ($20-$50 part)
  • Grinding: Bearings failing or sand in pump‚Äîneeds pump replacement soon
  • Water hammer (banging pipes): Missing or failed check valve‚Äîinstall water hammer arrestor

‚ö†Ô∏è Note: Unusual pump sounds often indicate imminent failure. If your pump is making noise, have it inspected soon to avoid emergency replacement (which often costs more).

10. Intermittent Water Loss

üîç Symptoms

Water works fine most of the time but occasionally runs out, especially during heavy use or hot afternoons

This frustrating problem usually indicates your well's recovery rate can't keep up with demand during peak usage.

Solutions by Severity

  1. Mild: Stagger water usage (don't run irrigation, laundry, and showers simultaneously)
  2. Moderate: Install a storage tank system that fills slowly and supplies household during peak demand
  3. Severe: Well deepening or hydrofracturing to improve yield
  4. Persistent: May need to drill a new, deeper well in a better location

üí° Pro tip: Check how deep other wells in your area go. Our Well Lookup Tool shows depths of 900,000+ California wells. If your well is shallower than nearby wells, deepening could help.

When to Call a Professional

Some well problems are DIY-friendly, but others require professional equipment and expertise:

DIY-appropriate:

  • Checking/resetting breakers
  • Replacing sediment filters
  • Adjusting pressure tank air charge
  • Replacing pressure switch
  • Basic water testing

Call a professional for:

  • Pump replacement or repair
  • Well inspection (camera work)
  • Well deepening or modification
  • Electrical issues beyond breaker
  • Persistent water quality problems
  • Complete water loss

Prevention: Annual Well Maintenance

Most well problems can be prevented or caught early with simple annual maintenance:

  1. ‚úì Test water quality (bacteria, nitrates minimum)
  2. ‚úì Inspect wellhead for damage or pest intrusion
  3. ‚úì Check pressure tank air charge
  4. ‚úì Clean or replace sediment filters
  5. ‚úì Listen for unusual pump sounds
  6. ‚úì Document water pressure and flow rate
  7. ‚úì Clear vegetation around wellhead

Spending 30 minutes annually on maintenance can prevent expensive emergency repairs and extend your system's life by years.
