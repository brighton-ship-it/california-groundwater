When to Replace Your Well Pump: 7 Warning Signs

Updated January 2025 ‚Ä¢ 10 min read

Your well pump is the heart of your water system. When it fails, you're without water‚Äîand emergency replacement costs significantly more than planned replacement. Here's how to recognize when your pump is failing and what to expect when it's time for a new one.

How Long Should a Well Pump Last?

Well pump lifespan depends on the type, usage, and water quality:

Pump Type Typical Lifespan Factors Affecting Life
Submersible pump 8-15 years Depth, sand content, cycling frequency
Jet pump (shallow well) 10-15 years Run time, pressure settings
Jet pump (deep well) 8-12 years Depth, water table fluctuation
Pressure tank 10-15 years Cycling frequency, water quality

Key insight: If your pump is over 10 years old and showing any warning signs, replacement is often more cost-effective than repair. Repair costs can be 50-75% of replacement, and a repaired old pump may fail again soon.

7 Warning Signs Your Pump Needs Replacement

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #1: Frequent Cycling (Short Cycling)

Your pump turns on and off repeatedly‚Äîevery few seconds to minutes‚Äîeven when water isn't being used heavily. This is often the first sign of trouble.

What's happening: Usually indicates a failing pressure tank bladder, but can also mean the pump is struggling to maintain pressure.

Action: Check pressure tank first (cheaper fix). If tank is fine, pump may be wearing out.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #2: Decreased Water Pressure

Gradual decline in water pressure over months. Showers feel weaker, sprinklers don't reach as far.

What's happening: Pump impellers are worn, reducing the pump's ability to push water.

Action: If pump is under 8 years old, may be repairable. Over 8 years, consider replacement.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #3: Air Sputtering from Faucets

Air bursts mixed with water, especially when first turning on a faucet.

What's happening: Pump may be drawing air due to worn seals, or water level has dropped below the pump intake.

Action: Have well inspected. May need pump lowered or replaced with deeper-rated pump.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #4: Sandy or Sediment-Filled Water

Water suddenly becomes sandy or you notice sediment in toilet tanks and at faucet aerators.

What's happening: Pump is pulling sand from the formation, often due to a failing screen or a pump set too low.

Action: Sand destroys pump internals quickly. Address immediately to prevent pump failure.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #5: Unusually High Electric Bills

Significant increase in electrical usage without other explanation.

What's happening: Worn pump runs longer to deliver the same water, or is cycling excessively.

Action: A new, properly-sized pump often pays for itself in energy savings within a few years.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #6: Strange Noises

Grinding, clicking, or loud humming from the well area or pressure tank.

What's happening: Bearings failing, motor struggling, or electrical problems.

Action: Strange sounds usually mean failure is imminent. Plan replacement soon.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sign #7: Pump Runs Constantly

You can hear the pump running even when no water is being used.

What's happening: Either a leak somewhere in the system, or pump can't build enough pressure to shut off.

Action: First check for leaks. If no leaks, pump is likely failing.

Repair vs. Replace: Making the Right Choice

Use this guide to decide whether to repair or replace:

Consider REPAIR if:

  • Pump is less than 5 years old
  • Problem is electrical (capacitor, pressure switch)
  • Issue is in accessible components (pressure tank, controls)
  • Repair cost is less than 40% of replacement cost

Consider REPLACEMENT if:

  • Pump is over 8-10 years old
  • Multiple components are failing
  • Repair requires pulling the pump from the well
  • Same problem has occurred before
  • Pump is undersized for current needs
  • Energy costs are excessive

Choosing the Right Replacement Pump

When replacing your pump, consider these factors:

1. Match the Well Depth

The pump must be rated for your total pumping depth (well depth minus static water level, plus vertical rise to pressure tank, plus friction losses).

2. Calculate Required Flow Rate

Most California homes need 5-10 gallons per minute (GPM). Calculate based on:

  • Number of bathrooms (√ó 1.5 GPM each)
  • Kitchen (1 GPM)
  • Laundry (2 GPM)
  • Outdoor irrigation (varies widely)

3. Consider Variable Speed Pumps

Variable speed (constant pressure) pumps cost more upfront but offer:

  • Consistent water pressure regardless of demand
  • Reduced energy costs (30-50% savings)
  • Less wear from cycling
  • Longer pump life

4. Don't Oversize

Bigger isn't better. An oversized pump:

  • Cycles more frequently
  • Wears out faster
  • Costs more to operate
  • Can damage your well (pulls sand)

The Replacement Process

Here's what to expect when having your pump replaced:

  1. Initial assessment: Contractor inspects well and current system
  2. Quote: Written estimate including pump specifications
  3. Scheduling: Most jobs can be completed in one day
  4. Pump removal: Old pump is pulled from well (takes 1-3 hours depending on depth)
  5. Inspection: Well condition is assessed, water level measured
  6. Installation: New pump is installed and connected
  7. Testing: System is tested for flow rate and pressure
  8. Startup: Pressure tank is set and system is started

Total time: Usually 4-8 hours for a straightforward replacement.

Finding a Qualified Contractor

For pump replacement in California:

  • License required: C-57 (Well Drilling) license for any work inside the well
  • Get multiple quotes: At least 2-3 estimates
  • Verify insurance: Contractor should have liability and workers' comp
  • Ask about warranty: Most pumps have 1-5 year manufacturer warranty; ask about labor warranty

Extending Your New Pump's Life

Make your new pump last longer with these practices:

  • ‚úì Install a sediment filter (change every 3-6 months)
  • ‚úì Maintain proper pressure tank air charge
  • ‚úì Fix any leaks promptly (reduces cycling)
  • ‚úì Install a cycle stop valve or constant pressure system
  • ‚úì Protect from power surges with a pump protector
  • ‚úì Don't run the well dry (if water runs out, stop and let it recover)
  • ‚úì Annual system check by a professional

A well-maintained pump in good water conditions can last 15+ years. Neglected pumps in sandy or corrosive water may fail in under 5 years.
